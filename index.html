To whoever takes this course next,

I walked into this course thinking it was going to be an easy Global Studies course. I walked out with one TikTok video at 960k views and counting, a completely different relationship with AI, and a few things about myself I genuinely did not know before. Here is what actually happened.

Part 1 — Most Impactful Learning Experiences

The moments that changed something

About myself: The Tahrir trip is where it started. We were meeting students who came to Egypt just to learn Arabic, and I remember feeling this sense of pride, like wow, someone actually wants to learn my language. Then I tried to speak to them in Arabic and I could not get through a single sentence without throwing in English words. I have spoken Arabic my entire life. I grew up with it. And I had never once noticed that I could not speak it cleanly on its own. That bothered me. So I started practicing, one minute, one topic, fully in Arabic with zero English. I posted videos just to see what would happen. Second video hit 960k views now. I went from 300 followers to 3,200 in less than a month. That one trip did not just teach me something about culture. It showed me a gap in myself I had never seen before, and closing that gap turned into something real.

For my academic life: The AI literacies module changed how I actually use AI day to day. The big idea was the difference between prompting and dialogue, and once I understood it I could not unsee it. A single prompt and a polished output is the lazy version. The real learning happens when you stay in the conversation, push back, catch where the AI is wrong, and force it to be clearer. I stopped using AI like a vending machine after this module.

For my social life: Soliya was the only time this semester where I was the only guy in the room. At some point the conversation went toward wellbeing and mental health, and I said what I actually thought: that men's mental health gets ignored and it matters. I said it expecting it to be awkward. It was not. Everyone agreed immediately. That moment stayed with me because I almost did not say it. The format of Soliya, no raised hands, no waiting for your turn, just jumping in, was something I had to get used to. By the end it felt more natural than the way I normally communicate. I use that now.

"Jumping into a conversation without raising your hand is not rude. It is participation. That sounds obvious. It was not for me."

For my career: The AI in Your Profession assignment was the one that surprised me the most. My whole assumption going in was that AI barely touches construction engineering because we are an on-site, physical industry. Both people I interviewed completely dismantled that. Dr. Mohamed Abdallah was using ML for water demand forecasting and environmental monitoring. My former TA Eng. Bassel Harby had built SetoutAI, a tool that takes a set of land plots and automatically figures out which ones give you the highest return and what to build on them. I did not know any of this existed. The gap between what AI can do in my field and what is actually being used right now is massive. That gap is where the opportunity is, and I am seriously thinking about whether there is a startup in there somewhere after I graduate.

Part 2 — Three Things I Created

Not the three things I spent the most time on. The three that actually meant something.

01

First Week of Corporate — The Game ↗

I built a narrative game with 12 scenarios pulled from my own first weeks at Siemens. I ran a simulator with 10,000 random playthroughs to make sure all six endings were actually reachable. I checked every single color pair against the WebAIM Contrast Checker for accessibility. I rewrote scenarios multiple times because the first versions had an obviously correct answer and that kills the whole point of a choice-based game. This was the first thing I made this semester that I genuinely wanted other people to play, not just submit.

02

Soliya Connect Reflection ↗

This is not my best writing. It is my most honest writing. I talked about immigration, men's mental health, and the fact that I cannot speak Arabic without mixing in English. None of those were easy things to put on a public blog. I put them there anyway. That was the assignment in a way.

03

AI in Your Profession — Construction Engineering ↗

I went in with one assumption and left with the opposite one. I interviewed a professor and a startup founder. Both of them showed me applications of AI in construction I had never heard of. I also pushed back in my reflection on an Anthropic study that claimed minimal AI coverage in my field, because my own interviews said otherwise. That is probably the most critical thinking I did all semester.

Part 3 — What I Would Change

Honest feedback for the course

1

Start Soliya in Week 2, not mid-semester. The conversations get good once people are comfortable with each other, and that takes time. Starting it early gives those conversations somewhere to go. Also, making it one hour instead of 2 would be better.

2

Add at least one heavy group assignment. Almost everything we submitted was individual work. The course builds real connection in the classroom through activities and the screen setups, but that could get carried into actual graded work. A group project with real weight would change that.

3

Reduce the time taken to complete the AI in Your Profession assignment or turn it into something shorter in class. I loved the idea though.

Take this course if you think you already understand technology and you want to find out you do not.

What I used, what I would keep — tap each card to expand

reflect.creativitycourse.org

Liked

First time I had ever blogged publicly.

It forced me to write for a real audience, not just a professor. It made me think more carefully about what I was saying. However, I don't like the design that much. It can have a full Gen Z look, cleaner, faster, more visual.

+

Classroom smart screens

Liked

6 screens, groups, real connection.

The setup with 6 screens and rotating groups forced me to actually talk to people I had never spoken to in the whole semester. At some point someone wrote anonymously that I made them feel heard. I did not expect that and it stuck with me. I will use this kind of collaborative setup in any workshop or event I run in the future.

+

WebAIM Contrast Checker

Liked

Accessibility I had never thought about.

Before this course I had genuinely never thought about color accessibility. Then I had to run 12 color pairs through the WCAG contrast formula for my game and it changed how I look at every slide deck and presentation I make. I still use it now. It takes 30 seconds and there is no reason not to. Link: webaim.org/resources/contrastchecker

+

Slack

Liked

Felt like Discord. Lowered every barrier.

It felt exactly like Discord which I already use with friends for gaming, so I was comfortable with it immediately. What mattered most was being able to message the professor in a way that felt direct without being weird. That channel is literally what got me a recommendation letter for the Parents Association Cup. I passed the first round and the finals are June 1.

+

QuickDraw (Google AI)

Mixed

The AI never guessed my drawings right.

I assumed I was just bad at drawing. My professor reframed it completely: what if the AI is the problem? What if it was trained on a different way of drawing things than how I drew them? That one reframe is now how I approach any AI output I am not sure about. I do not assume I am wrong first anymore.

+

Elicit

Liked

Research without needing perfect keywords.

I used it once in a session and immediately saw the value. You can find relevant research papers without needing the perfect search terms, get summaries targeted to your specific question, and pull structured data out of PDFs. I will use this for any research I do later on. The only thing holding it back is the interface. It looks outdated and that is enough to make most students ignore it.

Part 6 — Intercultural Experiences

Soliya, Tahrir, Equity Unbound

Soliya was genuinely one of the best experiences I have had at AUC. Talking to people from completely different countries about immigration, mental health, and how we see the world was something I did not know I needed. The format pushed me in ways a classroom never does. What would have made it better: start it earlier and cut the session length. Two hours is too much. The energy drops and everyone knows it.

The Equity Unbound session on how faculty should be using AI was something I had never even thought about before walking in. I always thought about AI responsibility from the student side. Sitting in a breakout room and working through the same scenario with people from different roles showed me how differently people think about the same problem depending on where they sit. I wanted more time in those rooms. The transition back to the full group always happened right when things were getting interesting.

The Tahrir trip I already talked about in Part 1. The one thing that would have made it better is more preparation. If someone had told me in advance that I would only be speaking Arabic, I would have had time to mentally prepare. The surprise was useful in the end or maybe I understood it differently.

With full honesty,
