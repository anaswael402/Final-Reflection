Part 1 — Most Impactful Learning Experiences

The moments that changed something

About myself: The Tahrir trip did something I didn't expect. We met students from abroad who had come to Egypt specifically to learn Arabic. I was excited, even a little proud, that someone wanted to learn my language. But the moment I opened my mouth, I couldn't get a full sentence out without dropping in English words. I had spoken Arabic my whole life and never noticed I couldn't do it cleanly. That hit hard. I went home and started a practice: one minute, one topic, fully in Arabic, no English. I posted the first video as an experiment. It passed 960,000 views. I went from 300 to over 3,200 followers. The Tahrir trip didn't teach me about intercultural exchange in theory — it exposed a blind spot I had about myself, and that exposure became the foundation of something real.

For my academic life: The AI literacies module, specifically the distinction between prompting and dialogue, changed how I approach every tool I use academically. The idea that a multi-turn conversation with an AI teaches you more than a single polished output flipped my default behavior. I stopped treating AI like a vending machine. The module made me realize that the learning happens in the back-and-forth — in the moments where I push back, catch an error, or force a clearer answer. That is a habit I will carry through my thesis and every research task going forward.

For my social life: Soliya. I was the only male in my group. At some point in a conversation about wellbeing, I said what I actually thought: that men's mental health gets ignored and it matters. I said it knowing it might land awkwardly. It didn't. Everyone agreed. That moment taught me something about the cost of staying quiet when you're the minority voice in a room. The format of Soliya — no raised hands, no turns, just jumping in — was uncomfortable at first. By the end it felt natural. I carry that into every group conversation now.

"I also learned that jumping into a conversation without raising your hand isn't rude. It's participation. That sounds small. It wasn't."

For my career: The AI in Your Profession assignment surprised me more than anything else this semester. I went in assuming AI barely touched construction engineering. I was completely wrong. Dr. Mohamed Abdallah walked me through ML applications in water demand forecasting and environmental monitoring. My former TA, Eng. Bassel Harby, had built SetoutAI — a tool that automates real estate feasibility analysis across multiple land plots simultaneously. The gap between where AI is theoretically possible in my industry and where it is actually deployed is enormous. That gap is an opportunity. I now think seriously about whether an AI-assisted infrastructure planning startup is worth pursuing after graduation.

Part 2 — Three Things I Created

The work I'd show someone

01

First Week of Corporate — The Game ↗

Twelve scenarios pulled directly from my own experience at Siemens. I built a simulator that ran 10,000 random playthroughs to verify all six endings were reachable and balanced. I ran every color pair through the WebAIM Contrast Checker and rewrote scenarios multiple times to eliminate the "obviously correct" answer. This is the piece that showed me what it actually means to build something for someone else — not for a grade, but for a person sitting in front of a screen who you need to keep engaged.

02

Soliya Connect Reflection ↗

Not because it's the most polished thing I wrote, but because it's the most honest. I wrote about immigration, men's mental health, and code-switching in Arabic. None of those were safe or easy topics to put publicly on a blog. The act of writing them — and choosing not to soften them — was the point.

03

AI in Your Profession — Construction Engineering ↗

I interviewed a professor and a founder. My starting assumption — that AI barely exists in construction — was completely dismantled by both conversations. The reflection I wrote pushed back on an Anthropic study that claimed minimal observed AI coverage in engineering, using my own interview evidence to challenge it. That kind of critical engagement is what this course asked for throughout the semester.

Part 3 — What I Would Change

Honest feedback for the course

1

Start Soliya in Week 2, not mid-semester. The relationships you build there take time to develop into real conversations. Starting earlier would give those conversations room to actually go somewhere. Also: cap each session at one hour. Two hours is long enough that people start clock-watching, and the quality of the last 40 minutes drops noticeably compared to the first 60.

2

Replace some of the individual written assignments with group projects that carry real weight. The course builds genuine community through classroom activities, but the assignments are almost entirely solo. Heavier collaborative work would make those in-class connections mean something outside the room too, and would better reflect how real professional work actually happens.

3

Trim the AI in Your Profession assignment or restructure it as a shorter in-class activity. The assignment itself was valuable, but the total volume of graded work this semester was high. Introducing one structured in-class assessment as a lighter-weight alternative would reduce that load while still testing the same learning in a different mode.

Anyone who thinks a "digital literacies" course will be easy, and who needs to be proven wrong about themselves, their assumptions, and the technology they think they already understand.

Liked

QuickDraw (Google AI)

Mixed

The AI never guessed my drawings right.

I thought my drawings were bad. The professor reframed it: what if the AI is the one that's wrong? What if it learned on a different set of shapes than what I drew? That single reframe is now how I approach any AI output I'm unsure about. The tool itself is simple, but the lesson it unlocked was not.

+

Elicit

Liked

Research without perfect keywords.

Genuinely useful for finding relevant academic papers without needing exact keyword matches, summarizing takeaways specific to a question, and extracting structured data from PDFs. I will use it for my thesis. The interface is outdated and that is the main barrier to wider student adoption. A redesign would help it reach people who won't sit with anything that looks old.

Part 6 — Intercultural Experiences

Soliya, Tahrir, Equity Unbound

Soliya gave me a framework I didn't have before for how to exist in a global conversation. Not just the mechanics — jumping in without a raised hand, navigating disagreement with curiosity — but the emotional weight of being a minority voice and choosing to speak anyway. The immigration topic landed differently when I had to answer my own interview questions. What would have made it better: starting earlier in the semester, and shorter sessions. Two hours is too long to stay genuinely present.

The Equity Unbound session on AI and faculty responsibility introduced something I had genuinely never considered: that professors and institutions have their own obligations around how they use AI, not just students. The breakout room structure worked well. Hearing how people in different roles processed the same scenario showed me how much context shapes ethical reasoning. What I would have changed: more time in the breakout rooms before returning to plenary. The transition back always felt too fast, just as the conversation was getting somewhere real.

The Tahrir trip I've described in Part 1. What would have made it better: more preparation beforehand. Knowing in advance that I would be speaking only Arabic — even 30 minutes of thinking about it — would have let me be more present instead of caught off guard. The surprise turned out to be useful. But intentional design would have been better than accidental discovery.

With full honesty,
